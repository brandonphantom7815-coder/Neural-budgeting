The Research

This isn't a vibe.
There's science behind it.

The framework behind this tool is built on peer-reviewed neuroscience. Here's what the research actually says.

RESEARCH 01 — BEHAVIORAL SYSTEMS
Your brain runs two competing systems, not one.
Neuroscientist Jeffrey Gray identified two fundamental motivational systems in the brain: the Behavioral Inhibition System (BIS), which responds to threat and punishment, and the Behavioral Activation System (BAS), which is driven by dopaminergic pathways and responds to reward and opportunity. Your balance between these two systems is measurable — and shapes everything from your risk tolerance to how much you procrastinate.
Carver, C. & White, T. (1994). Behavioral Inhibition, Behavioral Activation, and affective response to impending reward and punishment: The BIS/BAS Scales. Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 67(2), 319–333.

Gray, J.A. (1982). The Neuropsychology of Anxiety. Oxford University Press.
RESEARCH 02 — DELAY DISCOUNTING
How much you value the future is neurologically measurable.
Delay discounting — the tendency to prefer smaller rewards now over larger rewards later — is directly tied to dopamine function and prefrontal cortex connectivity. Research shows impulsivity measurably decreases as frontostriatal connections strengthen with age, explaining why adolescents and young adults show higher impulsivity than older adults. It also explains why overstimulation from high-frequency digital rewards makes long-term goals feel less motivating.
Olson, E.A. et al. (2015). Adolescent impatience decreases with increased frontostriatal connectivity. PNAS, 112(29).

Beckwith, H.M. (2017). The Role of the Medial Prefrontal Cortex in Delay Discounting. Indiana University–Purdue University Indianapolis.
RESEARCH 03 — DIGITAL OVERSTIMULATION
Chronic screen exposure reshapes your dopamine baseline.
The average person checks their phone 96 times daily — once every 10 minutes. Research shows that chronic exposure to high-frequency digital rewards alters baseline dopamine functioning in ways that parallel substance dependence. The prefrontal cortex, responsible for impulse control and long-term decision making, becomes less effective at overriding the reward circuit when it is constantly outpaced by rapid-fire stimulation.
Lu, Y. et al. (2022). Dopaminergic correlates of problematic smartphone use: A systematic review. Frontiers in Psychiatry, 13, 1072979.

Han, C. et al. (2021). Problematic smartphone use and cognitive control: A systematic review and meta-analysis. Addictive Behaviors, 118, 106886.

Dopamine Calculator

A realistic guide to how much is too much.

This isn't about telling you what to do. It's about showing you what the research says so you can make your own call. Every habit has a cost and a window where it stops working for you and starts working against you.

🌿
CANNABIS
Works for you up to a point.
Occasional use (1-2x per week) has minimal impact on baseline dopamine for most people. Daily use starts to downregulate your CB1 receptors — meaning you need more to feel the same effect, and without it, baseline motivation drops. Heavy daily use is directly linked to reduced dopamine release in the striatum.
SUSTAINABLE WINDOW
1-2 sessions per week with 2+ days off. Tolerance breaks every 4-6 weeks if using more frequently.
🥃
ALCOHOL
The rebound is the real cost.
Alcohol spikes dopamine in the short term then creates a sharp rebound below baseline — that's the anxiety and low motivation the next day. It's not just dehydration. It's your dopamine system in recovery. Drinking 3+ days per week consistently lowers your baseline over time and disrupts sleep quality which compounds the effect.
SUSTAINABLE WINDOW
1-2 occasions per week, with 4+ consecutive sober days. The day after always costs you something — budget for it.
🚬
NICOTINE / CIGARETTES
The trap is that it feels like it helps.
Nicotine releases dopamine, acetylcholine, and norepinephrine simultaneously — which is why it feels like focus and calm at once. The problem is your baseline adapts within days of regular use. You're no longer smoking to feel good, you're smoking to feel normal. The "calm" you feel is just withdrawal relief.
HONEST ASSESSMENT
There's no sustainable window for cigarettes. Occasional nicotine (non-combustible) has a much lower ceiling for harm. Your call — just know what you're trading.
🏋️
EXERCISE
The one habit that raises your ceiling.
Unlike most dopamine sources, exercise actually increases dopamine receptor density over time — meaning it makes everything else feel better too. Even a 20-minute walk raises dopamine and serotonin measurably. Strength training 3x per week has been shown in research to have antidepressant effects comparable to medication for mild-to-moderate cases.
SUSTAINABLE WINDOW
3-5 sessions per week. Rest days matter — that's when adaptation happens. More is not always better.
🍕
FOOD — CHEAT MEALS / DIET
Restriction without release doesn't work.
Hyper-palatable food (high fat + high sugar) triggers dopamine spikes similar to drug use in brain imaging studies. The issue isn't the cheat meal — it's the frequency. One intentional off-meal per week keeps you sane without derailing your baseline. Chronic restriction followed by binging creates the same boom-bust cycle as any other compulsive pattern.
SUSTAINABLE WINDOW
1 intentional cheat meal per week. Eat clean 6 days and actually enjoy the 7th — the anticipation is part of the reward mechanism.
🔬
MICRODOSING — PSYCHEDELICS
Emerging research. Real potential. Real unknowns.
Microdosing psilocybin (0.1–0.3g) or LSD (5–10mcg) has shown promising results in recent studies for creativity, focus, and emotional regulation. The Fadiman Protocol — one day on, two days off — is the most studied schedule. The mechanism isn't primarily dopaminergic; it works through serotonin 5-HT2A receptors and may promote neuroplasticity. Research is still early and legal status varies by location.
INFORMED PERSPECTIVE
If pursued: every 3rd day maximum, with monthly breaks. Set and setting matter. Not a substitute for the fundamentals — sleep, exercise, real connection.
This calculator is for informational purposes only and is grounded in published research. It does not constitute medical advice. Individual responses vary. The goal is honest information — not judgment, not restriction. You make your own calls.
